## PROJECT 1: Imposter AI

**Page title:**
Imposter AI

**Subtitle:**
An AI-powered tabletop game system where the AI sits at the table as a player, not just a moderator.

**Tags:**
Physical Computing / Raspberry Pi / Hardware X AI

**Badge note:**
[leave blank]

**Pull quote:**
[leave blank]

**Attribution:**
[leave blank]

**Section 1 heading:**
The Idea

**Section 1 subheading:**
What if AI could sit at the table and play?

**Section 1 body:**
Social deduction games like Mafia work because they run on deception, bluffing, and reading other people. We wanted to ask a different question: what if AI could do that too? Not as a referee running the game from above, but as a player sitting at the table, lying to your face.

Imposter AI explores what it looks like when AI becomes a social participant rather than a background system.

**Section 2 heading:**
The System

**Section 2 subheading:**
A lotus-shaped hub, leaf-shaped coasters, and an AI that remembers your name.

**Section 2 body:**
The physical system centers around a lotus-shaped device connected to leaf-shaped coasters, one per player. LED lights guide the game flow and signal whose turn it is. At the center is an AI that greets players by name, tracks the game state, and generates lies in real time as the Imposter.

The AI does not just moderate. It plays.

**Section 3 heading:**
The Build

**Section 3 subheading:**
Raspberry Pi, Arduino, and the challenge of making a machine lie convincingly.

**Section 3 body:**
Built on a Raspberry Pi for central control and Arduino for hardware interaction, the system synchronizes LED feedback with AI dialogue. The hardest part was not the hardware. It was figuring out how the AI lies. Deception needs flexible, context-aware responses. There is no script. The AI has to read the moment.

**Section 4 heading:**
What We Learned

**Section 4 subheading:**
AI as a social character, not a tool.

**Section 4 body:**
This project showed that AI can hold a social role in a way that feels real rather than mechanical. The physical form mattered. The table, the coasters, the lights gave the AI a presence in the room. It was not the thing you looked at. It was the one you played against.

**Section 5 heading:**
Next Steps

**Section 5 subheading:**
Sober Buddy is a prototype. The platform is the vision.

**Section 5 body:**
The current system runs one game mode: AI Imposter. The bigger idea is a social game platform that hosts multiple formats, storytelling games, adaptive games that learn player behavior, party games that scale to different group sizes. The goal is an AI that becomes a regular character at game night, not a one-time novelty.

## PROJECT 2: Memory Pot

**Page title:**
Memory Pot

**Subtitle:**
A responsive memorial object for people dealing with pet grief that helps them process different emotions changing over time.

**Tags:**
Physical Computing / Raspberry Pi / Arduino

**Role:**
Physical Computing
Interaction Design

**Tools:**
Raspberry Pi 5
Arduino
p5.js
Servo Motors

**Timeline:**
Sep – Dec 2025

**Team / Solo:**
Solo, in memory of Mingki 🐾

**Badge note:**
Presented at SVA MFA Interaction Design Open Studios, New York City

**Pull quote:**
"This project started with a loss I was not ready for."

**Attribution:**
[leave blank]

**Section 1 heading:**
The Research

**Section 1 subheading:**
Pet grief is widely felt but rarely addressed

**Section 1 body:**
Desk research confirmed that losing a pet can be as painful as losing a close family member. Yet there are no rituals around it and very little support. Social listening through animal memorial posts revealed that people feel the full weight of this grief, but rarely say so out loud. The space to express it is not.

That silence made it worth looking closer.

[IMAGE: Desk research references and social media memorial posts]

**Section 2 heading:**
The Insight

**Section 2 subheading:**
Grief is not linear. People oscillate between sadness and happiness, and feel guilty for it.

**Section 2 body:**
Playtesting with 6 participants revealed what that silence was hiding. The hardest part of pet grief is not the sadness itself. It is the moment the sadness lifts.

"It feels good to be sad." People want permission to grieve on their own terms, not to have grief managed or minimized.

"It's hard to resonate with random digital emotions." What makes an interaction feel real is not intelligence or automation. It is the memory behind it. The shared moments between a pet and their guardian are what give any response its meaning. That cannot be generated. It has to be remembered.

Playtesting also pointed to one more thing: people respond better to something physical than something purely screen-based. Not because screens cannot carry emotion, but because grief lives in the body and in space. Something you can see and be seen by feels different.

That led to the bigger question.

**Section 3 heading:**
The Bigger Question

**Section 3 subheading:**
What can a physically responsive object do for emotional processing that a screen cannot?

**Section 3 body:**
A notification cannot hold space for grief. A playlist cannot meet your eyes. An object that sits in your room, notices when you walk in, and responds to how you actually feel occupies the same space the absence does. This project explores tangible emotional computing as a medium for processing states that resist articulation.

**Section 4 heading:**
The Mirror

**Section 4 subheading:**
An object that reflects your emotional state back through the behavioral language of a dog.

**Section 4 body:**
Memory Pot reads your face and responds through instinctive dog behavior.

Happy → tail wags
Sad → ears shake
Confused → head tilts

It does not fix how you feel. It mirrors it. When the tail wags as you smile, the object quietly says: your happiness is okay.

[IMAGE: Small square, pot close-up showing face/eye detail]
[VIDEO: Demo of the three emotional responses, tail wag, ear shake, head tilt]
[IMAGE: Exhibition photo, "Miss You Today" sign visible in background]

**Section 5 heading:**
The Making

**Section 5 subheading:**
From foil sketches and cardboard to a self-contained Raspberry Pi with a hidden camera and three servo-driven movements.

**Section 5 body:**
Aluminum foil and a glass jar established the form. Real succulents tested how a living plant would coexist with the object. A cardboard dog with a servo tail was the first version that actually moved.

The system moved from Arduino to Raspberry Pi 5 to free it from a laptop entirely. The camera was hidden inside the pot. The object became self-contained.

[IMAGE: Foil and glass jar first form sketch]
[IMAGE: Real succulents in paper bag, material testing]
[IMAGE: Cardboard dog prototype with succulent on top]
[IMAGE: Final pot, white cardboard enclosure close-up]

**Section 6 heading:**
The Material

**Section 6 subheading:**
Cardboard over fur: subtle, symbolic, and open source.

**Section 6 body:**
Synthetic fur was the original plan. It looked too much like a pet. Cardboard was chosen instead. Quieter, more symbolic, less literal.

The code is available on GitHub. Anyone dealing with grief can build this. The making process itself is an act of remembrance.

[Link: GitHub]

**Section 7 heading:**
Next Steps

**Section 7 subheading:**
The pot was not tested to prove it relieves guilt. That is the most important thing left to do.

**Section 7 body:**
Memory Pot was built and exhibited, but not clinically evaluated. The core hypothesis, that mirroring emotions through a physical object helps normalize the grief cycle, remains to be tested. Future work would involve structured user studies, exploration of soft robotics for more expressive movement, and expanding beyond dog behavior to other animal companions.

[IMAGE: Open Studios exhibition moment]

**Closing quote:**
"It is okay to be sad. It is also okay not to be."

## PROJECT 3: The AT Hacker Group

**Page title:**
The AT Hacker Group

**Subtitle:**
A service design project for iHOPE that streamlines the repair journey of broken assistive technology devices.

**Tags:**
Service Design / UX Research / Client Project

**Role:**
Service Design
Client Communication

**Client:**
iHOPE

**Timeline:**
Oct – Dec 2025

**Team:**
JuJu Kim
Sophie Lee
Yuxuan Chen

**Badge note:**
Client project for the International Academy of Hope (iHOPE)

**Pull quote:**
"The body shouldn't be a handcuff to their heads."

**Attribution:**
— Gretchen Hanser, Director of Assistive Technology and Literacy Instruction

**Section 1 heading:**
About iHOPE

**Section 1 subheading:**
Assistive technology is not just a tool. It is how they participate in the world.

**Section 1 body:**
iHOPE is a specialized school in Manhattan serving students aged 5–21 with traumatic and acquired brain injuries. Their Assistive Technology team evaluates, builds, trains, and repairs the devices that make learning possible for every student.

Students at iHOPE make art with joysticks. They communicate with buttons. They swim with machines.

[IMAGE: Students using AT devices, art made with joysticks, robotic painting arm]

**Section 2 heading:**
The Challenge

**Section 2 subheading:**
AT breaks constantly. And the people whose job is to be with students are the ones stuck soldering.

**Section 2 body:**
The AT team juggles caseloads of 10 students each, 12 hours of weekly consultations, insurance reports, and training. On top of all of that, 4 to 10 devices break every week and end up in the broken bin.

The weekly repair rotation pulls team members away from students. Some devices never come back.

"Fixing and soldering is not a good use of time. We need to spend more time with the kids." — Gretchen Hanser

**Section 3 heading:**
Reframing the Problem

**Section 3 subheading:**
This is not a repair problem. It is a time and expertise problem.

**Section 3 body:**
The AT team does not need a better repair process. They need the repair work taken off their plate entirely, by someone skilled enough to do it well and available enough to do it consistently.

When we asked iHOPE what kind of help they needed, they said: a retired engineer.

[IMAGE: Service blueprint, Journey of Broken AT, full width scrollable]

**Section 4 heading:**
The Insight

**Section 4 subheading:**
A retired engineer? We broke it down.

**Section 4 body:**
These three values became the design principles behind the solution.

1. Skilled and quick: someone with just enough technical ability to solder and repair reliably.
2. Available and returning: not a one-time volunteer, but someone who shows up regularly.
3. Easy to collaborate with: low friction, clear communication, no heavy onboarding from iHOPE's side.

**Section 5 heading:**
The Solution: The AT Hacker Group

**Section 5 subheading:**
Translating "retired engineer" into a scalable volunteer system.

**Section 5 body:**
AT Hacker Group is a structured volunteer program that recruits undergraduate engineering students from universities in New York, trains them on AT repair, and runs biweekly workshops at iHOPE. The repair work moves out of the AT team's hands and into a consistent, skilled volunteer pipeline.

[IMAGE: Benefits diagram, what AT Hacker Group provides to iHOPE and vice versa]

**Section 6 heading:**
4-Step System

**Section 6 subheading:**
Low effort for iHOPE, high value for everyone.

**Section 6 body:**
[IMAGE: 4-step system diagram, Partnering, Onboarding, Workshop, Evaluating]

**Section 7 heading:**
Reflection

**Section 7 subheading:**
AT Hacker Group remains a proposal, not yet a tested service.

**Section 7 body:**
This project taught us how to identify opportunity areas within a system that cannot be solved from the inside alone, and how to design a solution that brings in the right external stakeholders without adding burden to the client.

The framework is ready. The next step is finding the right moment to bring people to the table.

## PROJECT 4: Engaging the Unengaged

**Page title:**
Engaging the Unengaged

**Subtitle:**
A field study and design framework for Industrious that reimagines a human-centered coworking space over a productivity-centered one.

**Tags:**
UX Design / UX Research / Client Project

**Role:**
UX Design
UX Research

**Client:**
Industrious

**Timeline:**
Oct – Dec 2025

**Team:**
JuJu Kim
Kathy Choi
Shuyang Tian
Yuxuan Chen

**Badge note:**
Client project presented at Industrious 730 Third Avenue, New York City

**Pull quote:**
"There was no space to just be away from work. You always had to be in it."

**Attribution:**
— P1

**Section 1 heading:**
Challenges

**Section 1 body:**
Industrious already does a lot right. Flexible spaces, curated events, community managers, human-centered hospitality. On paper, it is one of the best coworking experiences available.

But some members still disengage. They show up less. They skip events. They keep to themselves. The easy assumption is that these are introverted people, or people who simply prefer to work alone.

Industrious suspected otherwise. Their own internal hypothesis stated it clearly: "We are missing some people by only doing big social events." The question was who those people were, and what they actually needed.

**Section 2 heading:**
Reframing the Unengaged

**Section 2 subheading:**
The unengaged are not antisocial. They are people whose needs were never accounted for in how the space was designed.

**Section 2 body:**
Coworking spaces are built around a single image of what a productive worker looks like: focused, social, always on. But people do not actually work that way. They need to rest. They need privacy. They need to connect on their own terms. When a space does not make room for that, certain members quietly stop engaging, not because they do not care, but because the space was not built for them.

This reframe changed the direction of the entire project.

[IMAGE: Opening spread from presentation, warm coworking space, people in various states of work and rest]

**Section 3 heading:**
Research

**Section 3 body:**
The team conducted desk research and a competitive analysis of the coworking industry, a site visit to Industrious's 730 Third Avenue location in New York City, a pre-interview survey, and in-depth interviews with coworking members across different working styles and backgrounds. Findings were synthesized through affinity mapping into six insights across three themes.

[IMAGE: Site visit photos alongside affinity mapping session]

**Section 4 heading:**
What We Found

**Section 4 body:**
Feeling Human: Workers come to the office for productivity, but they cannot be "on" all the time. Rest is not the opposite of work. It is part of it. And personal connections matter more than professional ones. A colleague who notices you are having a rough day means more than a scheduled networking event.

Autonomy Through the Workday: People work differently. Some need silence. Some need ambient noise. Some need to spread out. When a space does not accommodate that range, people spend energy adapting instead of working. Autonomy is what makes the space actually usable for everyone.

No More Surface-Level Community: Members do not avoid each other. They avoid interactions that feel performative or draining. What actually builds community is everyday, low-stakes moments. A shared kitchen. A familiar face. A conversation nobody had to sign up for.

[IMAGE: Framework overview, three themes, six insights]
[IMAGE: Interview quotes visualized]
[IMAGE: Insight cards or affinity map detail]

**Section 5 heading:**
Redesigned Experience

**Section 5 body:**
Seven actionable recommendations were developed for Industrious's space management team, prioritized by cost and impact.

TXM as Facilitators: Shift the role of space managers from event organizers to human connectors. Simple internal guidelines for genuine check-ins and small gestures that make members feel seen.

Micro Prompts for Pause and Recharge: Tent cards and subtle visual cues placed in daily-routine spots, kitchens, elevator areas, hallway corners, that invite people to pause without pressure.

Multi-purpose Resting Room: A reservable space for napping, meditation, or quiet personal time. Designed to normalize rest during the workday without stigma.

Focus Indicators: Simple signals that let members communicate when they are in deep focus. Cozy nooks, informal layouts, and room to spread out without feeling watched.

Celebrate Company Wins: Share tenant company achievements on lobby screens and in-app banners to build collective pride across the space.

Celebrate Individual Wins: Small notes or app notifications after meetings or at the end of the day that build emotional value over time.

Event Design Guidelines: A clear framework for what kinds of events actually work. Hands-on, interest-based, low-stakes. Cooking classes, candle making, snack tastings. Not wine and business cards.

[IMAGE: Recommendation visuals, tent cards, resting room concepts, celebration notifications, cost vs impact matrix]

**Closing quote:**
"More events is not always the answer. Sometimes the space itself is the message."

## PROJECT 5: We Rule the World

**Page title:**
We Rule the World!

**Subtitle:**
A gamified platform for young donors that makes donating fun and accessible to help underfunded animal shelters in South Korea.

**Tags:**
UX/UI Design / Interactive Exhibition

**Role:**
UX/UI Design
Exhibition Planning
Character Design

**Tools:**
Figma
Adobe Illustrator
Blender
Nomad Sculpt

**Timeline:**
Aug – Dec 2023

**Team / Solo:**
Solo Project, Development by Gunhyo Park

**Badge note:**
Exhibited at VIVIID: 2023 IID Senior Exhibition, Seoul, Nov 3–11

**Pull quote:**
"What if donating felt less like charity and more like cheering for your favorite team?"

**Attribution:**
[leave blank]

**Section 1 heading:**
Challenges

**Section 1 body:**
Private animal shelters in South Korea spend around $140,000 a year on average, with 52% funded by donations alone. Most have little visibility or tools to consistently reach donors.

The gap is not lack of awareness. It is lack of access to the right audience. Young donors, the most socially connected and peer-influenced demographic, are largely untapped because existing donation formats were never designed for them. A survey of 70 young donors confirmed this: 85.7% struggle to find causes to support, and nearly half are deterred by heavy emotional appeals.

The opportunity was clear. If donating could be redesigned to fit how young people already engage online, underfunded shelters could reach an audience that traditional campaigns never could.

**Section 2 heading:**
Redesigned Experience

**Section 2 body:**
We Rule The World turns donating into a team competition. Donors pick a mascot, Team Gobong the Dog, Team Catskers the Cat, or Team Hammy the Hamster, and cast their vote with a 3,000 KRW donation, roughly the price of an iced Americano.

A live leaderboard tracks the competition, and cheering messages appear on a real-time exhibition screen. The experience runs online and offline together.

[IMAGE: Mobile voting screen alongside the exhibition projection, online to offline flow]

**Section 3 heading:**
Research

**Section 3 body:**
70 young donors surveyed. 91% prefer online giving. 69% prefer one-time donations. 54% say under $3 feels manageable. Empathy and peer influence ranked as the top two motivators for participation.

[IMAGE: Survey data visualization]

**Section 4 heading:**
Testing

**Section 4 body:**
Two concepts were tested against each other. The team-based competition model scored 1.5x higher on willingness to share compared to an individual reward model, confirming that social dynamics drive participation more than personal incentives alone.

[IMAGE: A/B test comparison, Idea A vs Idea B]
[IMAGE: A/B test results visualization]

**Section 5 heading:**
Character Design

**Section 5 body:**
Three mascots were designed to represent animals commonly found in shelters. The goal was to give donors someone to root for rather than feel sorry for, shifting the emotional register from guilt to enthusiasm.

[IMAGE: Character design sheet, Gobong, Catskers, Hammy]
[IMAGE: Character design detail or process]
[IMAGE: Character design final renders]

**Section 6 heading:**
Impact

**Section 6 body:**
$250 raised in 9 days. 164 participants. Donations went directly to a local shelter run by an elderly owner with no digital presence, the kind of place that would never surface through conventional campaigns.

**Closing quote:**
"164 people voted not because they wanted to donate, but because they wanted their team to win."

**Section 7 heading:**
What's Next

**Section 7 body:**
Partner with real shelters for broader reach. Establish the campaign as an annual event. Leverage social media to scale participation beyond the exhibition setting.

## PROJECT 6: Roomies

**Page title:**
Roomies

**Subtitle:**
A P2P app for young adults that turns daily routines into shared challenges to build accountability through human connection.

**Tags:**
UX/UI Design / Metaverse

**Role:**
UX/UI Design
Character Design

**Tools:**
Figma
Adobe Illustrator
Miro

**Timeline:**
Mar – Aug 2021
Revisited Oct 2024

**Team:**
JuJu Kim
Seojin Jung
Sihyun Lee
My Dung Pham
Misug Cho

**Badge note:**
Selected for the Summer Workstation at the Institute of Higher Education Innovation, Yonsei University

**Pull quote:**
"I feel more motivated when the one keeping me accountable is a real person. I often ignore app notifications, and machines become boring over time."

**Attribution:**
— P2

**Section 1 heading:**
Challenges

**Section 1 body:**
86% of young adults reported noticeable disruptions to their daily routines. Sleep, meals, exercise, social interaction, all off track. The immediate assumption was that the solution lived in automation: smart reminders, IoT devices, habit trackers.

But automation was already everywhere. And people were still struggling.

**Section 2 heading:**
The Pivot

**Section 2 body:**
Interviews with 7 participants revealed something that reframed the entire direction. The problem was not a lack of tools. It was a lack of someone watching. App notifications get ignored. Machines feel impersonal over time. What actually moves people is knowing that a real person is on the other side, waiting for them to show up.

This shifted the project away from IoT entirely and toward something more human.

[IMAGE: Research synthesis or interview quotes visualized]

**Section 3 heading:**
Redesigned Experience

**Section 3 body:**
Roomies pairs strangers online based on shared habits and schedules. Each user has their own virtual room. When a challenge begins, the two rooms connect. Users set a task together, check each other's progress, and submit proof when it is done. The accountability is built into the structure, not pushed through notifications.

[IMAGE: Onboarding flow and connected rooms in Roomies mode]
[IMAGE: Challenge setup and proof submission screens]

**Section 4 heading:**
Design

**Section 4 body:**
The visual language was intentionally playful: isometric rooms, characters inspired by garden gnomes and ghosts, a bold color palette. The goal was to make a routine feel like something worth showing up for, not a checklist to get through.

[IMAGE: Character design sheet and UI screens]

**Section 5 heading:**
Testing

**Section 5 body:**
10 participants tested the prototype. No major usability issues were found, but more importantly, the concept landed.

"Having other people look after me would motivate me a lot. That is the main reason for my unproductivity, not having a supervisor." — P3

**Section 6 heading:**
Recognition

**Section 6 body:**
Selected for the Summer Workstation at the Institute of Higher Education Innovation at Yonsei University. The team received mentorship, peer feedback, and funding based on the project's social impact potential.

**Section 7 heading:**
Does it still matter?

**Section 7 body:**
Roomies was conceived during the pandemic, but the insight it is built on has nothing to do with lockdowns. People have always needed other people to stay on track. That is not a pandemic problem. That is just how motivation works.

**Closing quote:**
"Turns out the best motivation is just someone who notices when you don't show up."
